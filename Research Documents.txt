Career Signal Loop + Career Twin Engine: A 28-Day Build & Strategy Plan for the Talentbank Tech Hackathon
TL;DR
Build a "navigation, not prediction" product around one genuinely defensible idea: a trajectory-evidence engine that shows the realistic range of outcomes for people with a similar "shape," powered by an honest hybrid of vector similarity + deterministic aggregation + LLM explanation over one shared data engine. No competitor in the Malaysian market does evidence-based trajectory navigation; LinkedIn Career Explorer, Eightfold, SkyHive and FutureFit AI are either enterprise-only, US/Europe-data-centric, or framed as prediction/recommendation.
Data is feasible in 28 days by combining O*NET (CC BY 4.0, US skills/transitions taxonomy) + ESCO (free reuse, occupation/skills graph) + DOSM OpenDOSM salary/wage data (CC BY 4.0) + Karrierewege/OpenResume open trajectory datasets + synthetic trajectory generation calibrated to Malaysian salary guides. DOSM gives authoritative skill-category and sector salary anchors; recruiter guides (Michael Page, Hays, Robert Walters) fill role-level detail.
The winning stack is Next.js + Supabase (Postgres + pgvector) + Claude/OpenAI, deployed live on Vercel, with one shared trajectory engine serving three views (candidate / employer / university). This maps directly onto the five judging criteria, especially Completeness (live URL, production-ready) and System Design (one engine, three surfaces).
Key Findings
The hackathon context
The Talentbank Tech Hackathon 2026 ("First Cohort") is a 28-day national build with MDEC institutional backing. The compulsory module is Career OS — a platform "that will guide professionals from their first profile at 16 to their final pivot at 65." It is explicitly AI-native (Cursor, Claude, Copilot, v0, Bolt, Replit Agent permitted; you declare which tools you used at submission). Judging "scores taste, integration, and output quality, not hand-typed code." Three written checkpoints fall at roughly days 10, 20, and 30. "Production-ready" is defined verbatim as: "Clean, documented, tested, and directly integrable without significant rework. A live demo URL is required. If the code couldn't survive our engineers' code review, it isn't production-ready." The stack is open (React/Next/Vue/Svelte/Angular all accepted), but note Talentbank's production platform runs on Angular — "We handle re-implementation where needed." On adoption, full IP transfers to Talentbank.
Strategic implication: because Talentbank re-implements in Angular, judges weight architecture clarity, data-model quality, and AI output quality above your specific framework choice. Lean into clean separation of concerns and a crisp, independently-callable shared data engine.
1. Competitive landscape & differentiation
What exists:
LinkedIn Career Explorer: maps a database of over 41,000 skills to more than 6,000 job titles, computing a "skills similarity" score (its canonical example: food server → customer service specialist = 71% similarity) and a "popularity" score for transitions derived from members' actual job-history changes. US-centric; surfaces single transitions, not multi-step trajectory ranges; built on proprietary LinkedIn data that is not licensable for your build. Important caveat: the original standalone tool (linkedin.github.io/career-explorer) appears to have been retired/folded into core LinkedIn product surfaces — verify current availability before describing it as "free, no login" in your pitch.
Eightfold AI: enterprise talent intelligence, per its own April 2026 press release "trained on 1.6 billion career trajectories and 1.6 million skills," scoring candidates "on a 1 to 5 scale using more than 50 variables." Founded 2016 (Santa Clara), used by 100+ organizations across 155 countries (Microsoft, PayPal, Starbucks, Chevron). Explicitly predictive and enterprise/ATS-facing — exactly the "black-box score" framing the hackathon warns against.
SkyHive (now part of Cornerstone): "Quantum Labour Analysis," reskilling navigator, enterprise workforce planning.
Lightcast (formerly Emsi Burning Glass): labour-market intelligence/skills data and APIs — infrastructure, not a consumer navigation product.
FutureFit AI: closest philosophical sibling — "GPS for your career," 350M+ global talent profiles (vendor claim), government/workforce-board facing, recommends skills-based pathways and roadmaps. Still framed around "recommend possible career paths" / roadmaps rather than an honest range-of-outcomes.
Faethm (now part of Pearson): automation/future-of-work risk modeling — predictive.
Malaysian/APAC: JobStreet (SEEK group) — listings + AI matching; Hiredly/WOBB — Gen-Z/millennial AI job matching ("Let the jobs find you"), ~1.3M jobseekers, AI resume-to-job summaries; Maukerja/Ricebowl — mass-market high-volume; MyNext (TalentCorp) — psychometric profiling + internship matching with a "Career Explorer" feature for students; TalentCorp MyMahir — critical occupations list + skills taxonomy. None present an evidence-based "people like you went here, with these trade-offs" trajectory landscape.
The gap and the differentiation (named clearly): Every incumbent does one of: (a) single-step skill-similarity suggestions (LinkedIn), (b) enterprise predictive matching/scoring (Eightfold/SkyHive), (c) job listings + matching (JobStreet/Hiredly), or (d) psychometric/interest profiling (MyNext). None shows the realistic distribution of multi-step trajectories for people of a similar shape, with honest trade-offs (salary range, time, probability mass, common detours), localized to Malaysia, framed as navigation. Your differentiator is the Career Twin Engine: it retrieves a cohort of anonymized trajectories matching the user's shape (skills + education + prior roles + geography) and surfaces the spread of where they went — not a single predicted destination. This is "evidence you can interrogate," not a black-box score. State this in one sentence in your pitch: "We don't predict your future; we show you, with evidence, the realistic landscape of where people who started where you are have actually gone — and the honest trade-offs between those paths."
2. Data sources for Malaysia (feasibility-ranked)
Tier 1 — Backbone (open, licensed, immediately usable):
O*NET Database (US Dept of Labor) — CC BY 4.0, free download in flat files/SQL. The ONET-SOC taxonomy contains 1,016 occupations, of which 923 are data-collection-level and 889 have full knowledge/skills/abilities data; includes "Related Occupations," Bright Outlook, education profiles. Use for: the skills taxonomy spine, skill-to-occupation links, and adjacency/related-occupation edges. Must display attribution and use "ONET" as an adjective (trademark rules).
ESCO (European Commission) — free reuse under Commission Decision 2011/833/EU; acknowledgement required ("This service uses the ESCO classification of the European Commission"). ~3,000+ occupations, 13,000+ skills, multilingual, downloadable CSV/RDF + web API (libraries under EUPL 1.2). Maps to ISCO. Use for: occupation graph, skill relations, and as the normalization target — both major open trajectory datasets already link to ESCO.
DOSM / OpenDOSM (open.dosm.gov.my; API at api.data.gov.my/opendosm) — CC BY 4.0 (verbatim on every dataset page). Confirmed granularity: salary/wage data by broad skill category (skilled/semi-skilled/low-skilled), by the 9 MASCO major groups, by sector (MSIC), state, sex, age, and education — not by detailed job title. Concrete citable figures from the DOSM Salaries & Wages Survey Report 2024 (released 29 Sep 2025): overall mean monthly salary RM3,652 (up 6.1% from RM3,441) and median RM2,793 (up 7.3% from RM2,602). By MASCO major group — mean: Managers RM7,121, Professionals RM6,524, Technicians & Associate Professionals RM4,077, low-skilled RM2,128 (up 7.4%); median: Managers RM5,990, Professionals RM5,821, Technicians RM3,541. By education (mean): Degree RM6,114, Diploma RM3,414. The formal-sector median wage was RM3,167 in December 2025 (a distinct quarterly administrative series — keep separate from the annual survey). From DOSM Graduates Statistics 2024 (released 1 Nov 2025): graduate median monthly salary RM4,521 (up 2.5% from RM4,409) and mean RM5,330 (up 8.1% from RM4,933); highest graduate medians by location — Kuala Lumpur RM5,888, Putrajaya RM5,723, Selangor RM5,207.
Tier 2 — Role-level salary realism (mind licensing):
Recruiter salary guides: Michael Page Malaysia 2026, Hays Asia 2025, Robert Walters Malaysia 2025/2026, Randstad Malaysia 2025 — role-by-experience RM ranges (e.g., Robert Walters 2025: junior software developers ~RM60k–120k/yr, seniors ~RM144k–216k/yr; JobStreet software engineer avg ~RM4,300–6,000/month; fresh-grad data analysts ~RM4,000–5,500/month). Licensing note: these are copyrighted marketing PDFs — do not scrape or redistribute tables; use published headline figures as calibration constants for synthetic data and cite them as named in-app sources.
TalentCorp MyMahir Critical Occupations List (MyCOL) 2024/2025 — 66 critical roles across sectors, built from the Labour Force Survey + Salaries & Wages Survey + online job-posting data, classified by MASCO. Use for: "demand signal" tags ("this destination is on Malaysia's Critical Occupations List") to ground the employer/university views in national priorities.
Tier 3 — Open trajectory datasets to seed the Career Twin Engine:
Karrierewege / Karrierewege+ (Senger et al., COLING 2025; Hugging Face/GitHub) — 500k+ career paths linked to ESCO with synthesized free-text titles. Caveat: German employment-agency origin → cultural bias; use as structure, re-weight to Malaysia.
OpenResume (Yamashita et al., IEEE BigData 2024; Zenodo) — anonymized + synthetic resumes, titles normalized to ESCO, with differential privacy. Explicitly research-only, not for commercial operations — acceptable for a disclosed hackathon demo.
JobHop (Ghent, 2025) — 1.67M ESCO occupation transitions from 361k Flemish resumes.
Tier 4 — Live job/salary signal (optional, free tier):
Adzuna API — free app_id/app_key; salary histograms, historical salary, regional data, categories; Malaysia coverage exists. Use for: live "current openings/salary distribution" context on a destination node.
LinkedIn data: not usable — scraping violates terms; Career Explorer data is served only via LinkedIn's own UI under its User Agreement. Do not build on it.
Synthetic trajectory generation (the pragmatic core): Because no large Malaysian trajectory dataset exists publicly, generate a calibrated synthetic cohort: take ESCO/O*NET occupation+skill structure and real transition probabilities from Karrierewege/JobHop as the shape of how careers move; calibrate salary nodes to DOSM skill-category/sector/education anchors and recruiter-guide role ranges; and use an LLM to expand each path into realistic Malaysian-context narratives (titles, employer-types, typical durations). This yields a defensible, honest, fully-owned dataset with no licensing risk, disclosed in-app as "modeled trajectories calibrated to Malaysian labour data."
3. Technical build plan (28 days)
Architecture — one engine, three surfaces ("Career Signal Loop"):
Next.js (App Router) front end ── 3 role views (Candidate / Employer / University)
        │
        ├── /api (Next API routes; optional thin FastAPI service for heavier ML)
        │
   Career Twin Engine (shared)
        ├── Embeddings: OpenAI text-embedding-3-small (1536-d, $0.02/1M tokens)
        ├── Vector store: Postgres + pgvector (Supabase) — HNSW index
        ├── Trajectory graph: same Postgres (adjacency tables) — no separate graph DB at demo scale
        └── LLM reasoning: Claude (Sonnet) for honest explanation + trade-off narratives;
            strict JSON / tool-use output + post-validator
        │
   Postgres (Supabase): occupations, skills, trajectories, salary_anchors, users
Why this stack:
Frontend: Next.js (React). Fastest path to a polished, deployable app; v0/Bolt can scaffold UI; deploys to Vercel in minutes with a live URL (directly satisfies Completeness). Angular is Talentbank's production target but they re-implement — React is faster to demo.
Backend: Next.js API routes for most logic; add a small Python/FastAPI microservice only if you want self-hosted sentence-transformers. For 28 days, calling hosted embedding + LLM APIs from Node keeps it simplest.
Database: Postgres + pgvector via Supabase, not Neo4j. At hackathon scale (thousands–tens of thousands of trajectories), pgvector with an HNSW index gives production-ready semantic similarity inside the same SQL database that holds relational trajectory/salary data — one system, simpler ops, "directly integrable." Neo4j's multi-hop graph-traversal advantage only matters at a scale you won't hit here; cite it as a roadmap option. Supabase also provides auth, row-level security, and an instant REST/realtime layer — strong signals for Code Quality/Security.
AI approach: hybrid — not pure-LLM, not pure-vector. (1) Embed the user's "shape" (skills + education + prior roles + geography as a structured text profile) with text-embedding-3-small. (2) pgvector cosine search retrieves the cohort of nearest anonymized trajectories. (3) Aggregate that cohort deterministically into the range — distribution of next roles, salary percentiles from DOSM/guide anchors, median time, % who took each branch. (4) Claude turns the aggregates into an honest, hedged narrative with explicit trade-offs and a "this is evidence from N similar paths, not a prediction" frame. This separation is what defeats the "black-box score" and "false precision" objections: the numbers come from transparent aggregation; the LLM only explains.
Embeddings choice: text-embedding-3-small is the safe default ($0.02/1M tokens, 1536-d, universally supported by pgvector). Indexing even 50k trajectory documents costs cents. Use Matryoshka truncation to 512-d if you want smaller vectors. Open-source models (BGE-M3, Jina v3) are only worth self-hosting above ~15M embeddings/month — irrelevant here.
LLM choice: Claude (Sonnet) for the explanation/reasoning layer — strong at structured analytical writing, instruction-following, and honest hedging. Note Claude can prepend prose before JSON, so enforce schema via tool-use/structured output and parse defensively. Keep a one-line provider abstraction so you can swap to GPT-4o/GPT-5 (which produces more predictable raw JSON by default) under rate-limit or latency pressure.
The interactive visualization (the "wow"): Use react-force-graph (vasturiano) or React Flow with a DAG layout (dagMode: 'lr') to render the branching trajectory landscape: the user's current node on the left, branching paths to the right; node size = probability mass (how many similar people went there), edge thickness = transition frequency, node color = salary band (DOSM-anchored), plus a badge for MyCOL "critical occupation." Clicking a node opens a side panel with the honest range (salary percentiles, typical time-in-role, common skills to bridge from O*NET, % of cohort). This single screen demonstrates Product/UX + AI Craft + System Design simultaneously.
Three demo modules sharing the engine:
Career Path Navigator (candidate): input shape → branching landscape + honest narrative + skill-bridge suggestions (O*NET skill gaps).
Smart Talent Matching (employer): input a role/skills → engine surfaces candidates whose trajectory shape fits the role's typical inflow, plus "adjacent talent" (people one bridge away) — reframing matching as evidence, not a score.
Lifelong Outcome Loop (university): aggregate cohort view — "students of programme X tend to land in these trajectory clusters; here are emerging destinations and skill gaps vs. MyCOL." Closes the loop by feeding outcome signal back to curriculum.
Phased 28-day timeline:
Days 1–5 (Foundation): Lock scope to three modules + one engine. Pull O*NET + ESCO; load into Postgres. Stand up Next.js + Supabase + Vercel (live URL from day 3). Define trajectory/salary schema. Ingest DOSM anchors.
Days 6–10 (Engine v1; Checkpoint 1 ~day 10): Build synthetic trajectory generator (ESCO/Karrierewege structure × DOSM/guide salary calibration × LLM narrative expansion). Generate embeddings, build pgvector HNSW index. Ship cohort-retrieval + deterministic aggregation. Checkpoint write-up: architecture + differentiation.
Days 11–17 (Candidate module + viz): Build the branching force-graph visualization and honest side-panel. Wire Claude explanation layer with JSON schema + validator. Implement the "navigation not prediction" UX copy and cohort-size/confidence framing.
Days 18–20 (Employer + University modules; Checkpoint 2 ~day 20): Reuse the engine for the other two views. Add auth + row-level security. Checkpoint write-up: system design + AI craft.
Days 21–25 (Hardening): Tests (aggregation unit tests, API integration tests), docs (README, architecture diagram, data-licensing/attribution page, AI-tools declaration), security pass (env secrets, RLS, input validation via zod, rate limits). Seed a polished demo dataset.
Days 26–28 (Polish; Checkpoint 3 ~day 30): Performance, empty/error states, demo script, recorded walkthrough, final live-URL verification.
4. Scoring alignment
Product & UX Thinking (30%) — the biggest lever.
Lead the experience with the honest frame: a visible "This shows where people with a similar shape have gone — evidence to navigate with, not a prediction" banner. This directly answers the anti-"we predict your future" warning and becomes your narrative spine.
The branching trajectory visualization with probability-weighted nodes and honest side-panels is the signature UX moment. Show trade-offs explicitly (higher-salary path vs. faster path vs. most-common path).
One coherent product story across three users (the Career Signal Loop) demonstrates product thinking beyond a single tool.
Keep agency central: every screen lets the user adjust their shape and re-explore; nothing is prescriptive.
System Design & Integration (25%).
The "one shared engine, three surfaces" architecture is the headline — provide a clean architecture diagram in the README.
The single Postgres+pgvector store unifies relational + vector data — argue this as a deliberate, production-pragmatic choice (vs. premature Neo4j/separate vector DB).
Clean, swappable provider abstraction for embeddings + LLM. Document the pipeline: sources → normalization to ESCO → synthetic calibration → embeddings → retrieval → aggregation → explanation.
Completeness — works end-to-end, live URL, production-ready (20%).
Deploy to Vercel + Supabase from day 3; keep the live URL green throughout. All three modules must work end-to-end on seeded data.
Meet their verbatim bar: clean, documented, tested, directly integrable. Provide one-command local setup, a seed script, and .env.example.
Because production is Angular, make the engine an independently callable API with a documented OpenAPI spec so it's "directly integrable without significant rework" regardless of front-end framework — call this out explicitly.
AI Craft — tool choice, prompt design, output quality (15%).
Justify the hybrid design (embeddings for retrieval, deterministic aggregation for numbers, Claude only for explanation) as the principled way to avoid false precision — this reads as sophisticated AI judgment, not "we called an LLM."
Ship your actual prompts in the repo with schema, few-shot examples, and a validator. Show how prompts enforce hedged, evidence-grounded language and forbid deterministic predictions.
Declare the AI build tools used (Cursor/Claude/v0/etc.) as required at submission.
Code Quality — naming, structure, docs, security, tests (10%).
TypeScript throughout; clear module boundaries (engine / api / ui / data); conventional commits.
Security: Supabase RLS, secrets in env, input validation (zod), rate limiting on AI endpoints, no PII in synthetic data.
Tests: unit tests for the aggregation math (the part that must not lie) and integration tests for the retrieval API; document coverage.
Thorough README + architecture doc + data-licensing/attribution page (O*NET, ESCO, DOSM CC BY 4.0 acknowledgements).
Recommendations
Commit to the honest frame as the product's spine, not a disclaimer. Build the aggregation→explanation split so the "range, not prediction" promise is architecturally true. Threshold to change course: if user testing shows people still read it as prediction, add an explicit cohort-size and confidence indicator on every node and replace point numbers with bands.
Ship a live URL by day 3 and never let it go red. Completeness is 20% and the easiest to lose; an early deployed skeleton de-risks the whole build.
Build the engine first, the three views second. The shared engine is your System Design story (25%) and your differentiation. Get cohort retrieval + honest aggregation working on synthetic data before polishing any UI.
Use synthetic-but-calibrated Malaysian data and disclose it. This sidesteps all licensing risk while staying credible. Calibrate to DOSM anchors (RM3,652 mean, MASCO-group means, RM4,521 graduate median) + recruiter-guide role ranges; cite sources in-app.
Invest disproportionately in the branching visualization. It is the single artifact that demonstrates three judging criteria at once and is what judges will remember.
If you add 1–3 teammates, split by surface, not by layer: one owns engine/data, one owns the candidate view + viz, one owns employer+university views, you own architecture + AI prompts + the narrative. This keeps the shared engine coherent.
Stage the AI provider decision: default to Claude Sonnet for explanation quality; keep a GPT-4o fallback behind your abstraction. Switch if you hit rate limits or need faster/cheaper inference under demo load.
Caveats
No large Malaysian career-trajectory dataset exists publicly. Your trajectory cohorts will be synthetic-calibrated, not empirical Malaysian resumes. Be transparent — it is honest and defensible for a hackathon, but a real limitation versus a production claim.
DOSM open data does not reach job-title granularity — only skill category, the 9 MASCO major groups, sector (MSIC), state, and education. Role-level salary realism depends on recruiter guides (copyrighted; use as calibration, do not republish tables).
Open trajectory datasets (Karrierewege, OpenResume, JobHop) are European/research-only and culturally biased toward their source countries; OpenResume explicitly bars commercial operational use. Fine for a disclosed demo, not for production without replacement.
Recruiter salary-guide PDFs are gated/copyrighted; only headline figures are publicly citable. Do not scrape.
Competitor capabilities are largely vendor claims (Eightfold's "1.6 billion career trajectories," FutureFit's "350M profiles," LinkedIn's "41,000 skills / 6,000 titles") — treat as marketing figures, not independently verified. Verify LinkedIn Career Explorer's current availability before relying on it as a live comparison; the standalone tool appears to have been retired.
IP transfers to Talentbank on adoption — don't build anything you intend to reuse commercially elsewhere.
Two DOSM salary series are easily conflated: the annual Salaries & Wages Survey (mean RM3,652 / median RM2,793 for 2024) and the quarterly formal-sector administrative series (median RM3,167 in Dec 2025, ~60% of formal employees). Keep them distinct in any demo. Per-MASCO-group means were corroborated against DOSM's own 2024 release; verify against the DOSM Salaries & Wages Survey 2024 PDF before quoting publicly.
